Mérida, Yucatán

A brief introduction to Merida's history

In 1542, Spanish conquerors founded and named the city Mérida after a Spanish city. To build the city, the Spanish used many of the carved Maya stones to construct their colonial buildings, including many buildings found today in the historic center of the city, including the main cathedral. Limestone is predominately white, which gave rise to Mérida's nickname: White City. By the late 19th century, Mérida began prospering from the production of henequén, an agave plant species native to southern Mexico and Guatemala. Growers became wealthy and built large homes primarily out of the local white limestone that still can be seen on Mérida's Paseo de Montejo.

Points of interest that you may want to check

  • Mayan World Museum: One of Mexico's most important museums, compromising four large permanent exhibitions with over 1100 relics.

  • Mérida Cathedral: Largest church in Yucatán, this building's interior boasts a rich décor, notable for ti's many references to the town's Mayan and colonial histories.

  • Museo Casa Montejo: One of the finest examples of Spanish colonial architecture in México. Inside, its large and handsome rooms house Museo Casa Montejo, an interesting museum set around two courtyards, which is furnished with antique pieces imported from Europe.

  • Historic Plaza Mayor (Plaza Grande): Both the commercial and cultural hub of Mérida, it's a good place to start a walking tour of the old city center. In addition to being surrounded by some of Mérida's most important buildings, it's also a very pleasant space to simply hang out, thanks to its shady palm trees, beautiful flower gardens, and fountains.
